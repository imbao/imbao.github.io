1. What should I learn next to become an Architect in the AI era? Explain me how I need to learn that?

Answer:
To transition from a Senior Full-Stack developer into a Software Architect, you need to shift your focus from "how to implement features" to "how to design resilient, scalable, and maintainable systems." Since you are aiming for a promotion, demonstrating an understanding of trade-offs and decision-making frameworks is crucial.

Given your background in Java and JavaScript, you have a solid foundation. To evolve in the "AI Era," you should focus on architectural patterns that handle data-intensive workloads and decision-making maturity.

What to learn next:
Patterns & Design Principles (specifically DDD and ACID/CAP): As an architect, you aren't just writing code; you are defining boundaries. Domain-Driven Design (DDD) is essential for managing complexity in large systems, and understanding the CAP theorem is non-negotiable when designing distributed systems that integrate AI/ML components.
Architecture (Microservices & Distributed Systems): AI services are rarely monolithic. You need to understand how to design loosely coupled systems where AI models can be swapped or scaled independently from your core business logic.
Working with Data: AI is powered by data. You should understand how to architect data pipelines and choose between SQL and NoSQL storage based on the access patterns of your AI-driven features.
How to approach your learning (7 hours/week):
The "Architect's Mindset" (3 hours): Focus on the Decision Making and Design & Architecture skills. Instead of just reading, pick a project you've worked on recently and draft an Architectural Decision Record (ADR). Write down why you chose a specific database or framework and what the trade-offs were.
Deep Dive into Patterns (2 hours): Study one pattern per week (e.g., CQRS or Domain-Driven Design). Map it to a real-world problem you see in your current company’s stack.
System Design Practice (2 hours): Use the System Design Roadmap to practice drawing high-level architectures. Don't worry about the code; worry about the flow of data, bottlenecks, and failure points.
